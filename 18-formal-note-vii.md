# [Editor’s Note: Corpus Preparation Fragment]

The following note appears after the saturation test fragment.

The previous pages have brought the sentence system to a dangerous threshold. The six operators have returned to the nine endings. The number fifty-four has appeared, but only as pressure. The next temptation would be to build the table directly.

The writer refuses that temptation.

This page begins the corpus discipline. A registry cannot be populated from confidence. It must be populated from sentences that have been gathered, preserved, inspected, challenged, and returned to. The sentence must arrive before the cell. The living use must arrive before the formal instrument.

The editor has placed this fragment here because it marks the first turn from formal pressure toward collected evidence.

## Formal Note VII
### The Sentences Must Be Gathered Before They Are Counted

The crossings are waiting.

That does not mean they may be filled.

A waiting space can deceive the hand. Once the space is visible, the hand wants to write inside it. Six operators. Nine endings. Fifty-four possible crossings. The shape is almost too ready.

But a ready shape is not the same as a lawful registry.

The table must not be built from hunger for completion.

It must be built from sentences.

This is the next refusal.

Do not fill the crossing before the sentence has entered.

Do not name the cell before the landing has been heard.

Do not let the number decide what speech is allowed to do.

The corpus must come first.

I use the word corpus carefully.

I do not mean a heap of examples.

I do not mean favourite sentences copied because they flatter the system.

I do not mean a display of clever cases.

A corpus is a body of sentences gathered under discipline so that their movements may be inspected by more than one mind.

The body matters.

One sentence is too lonely.

One sentence may be obedient.

One sentence may have been chosen because it already knows where I want it to go.

Many sentences can resist me.

Many sentences can show whether the operation survives beyond one subject.

Many sentences can show whether I am hearing the sentence or only hearing my expectation.

The corpus is therefore not decoration around the registry.

It is the ground beneath it.

If the registry comes without a corpus, it will be a map of my certainty.

That is not enough.

The sentence system must become shareable.

A shareable system needs sentences another person can inspect.

So the first rule of the corpus is preservation.

Keep the sentence.

Do not improve it too quickly.

Do not replace its ordinary wording with a cleaner version.

Do not correct its awkwardness before the awkwardness has been heard.

Do not remove the small words that may be doing hidden work.

A sentence may carry its operation in a word that looks disposable.

Only.

Still.

Already.

Again.

Just.

Now.

Even.

Because.

But.

These words may tilt the field.

If I clean them away, I may destroy the landing I was meant to study.

The second rule is context.

A bare sentence may be useful for training the ear, but a sentence with pressure is needed for judgment.

The same words can land differently.

It was only a joke.

Without context, I can hear a possible closure. The sentence may reduce the field and ask the listener to let the matter rest.

But with context, the sentence may become many things.

It may be repair.

It may be evasion.

It may be cruelty.

It may be panic.

It may be a clumsy attempt to reopen what has already been harmed.

It may be a false closure.

The words alone do not settle the landing.

The field matters.

So the corpus must keep enough surrounding pressure to make inspection possible.

Who spoke?

To whom?

After what?

In answer to what?

What was open before the sentence?

What did the sentence ask to close, open, connect, repair, gather, distinguish, or spread?

What did the listener inherit?

What did the listener resist?

The corpus does not need every fact.

No sentence can carry every fact, and no record can preserve the whole world around it.

But the corpus must keep enough field for the crossing to be answerable.

The third rule is ordinary priority.

Begin with ordinary sentences.

Not because ordinary sentences are simple.

They are not.

Begin there because ordinary speech hides the machinery most cleanly.

The marker sits on the table.

The road splits after the church.

The meeting starts at nine.

The door closed.

The key fits the lock.

The partners adjusted to each other's pace.

The signal reached farther than before.

The case is settled.

These sentences do not ask to be admired.

That makes them useful.

If the system works only on dramatic sentences, it has found drama, not speech.

If it works only on examples written to prove the system, it has found obedience, not law.

The ordinary sentence is the clean test because it has no interest in becoming theory.

It simply lands.

The fourth rule is variation.

A crossing must not be represented by one subject alone.

If distinction is tested only on roads, I may mistake road behaviour for sentence behaviour.

Let the subject change.

A road divides.

A committee divides.

A river divides.

A page divides into columns.

A family divides over the decision.

A field is divided by a fence.

A sentence divides the listener's attention.

Different worlds.

A repeated operation.

If the operation survives the changing subject, it begins to appear as operation.

This is saturation.

But saturation must not become accumulation without listening.

Ten examples do not prove a crossing if all ten were chosen to obey.

Variation must include resistance.

The fifth rule is objection.

Every corpus entry should allow the listener to say no.

No, that is not distinction.

No, that is not Toward.

No, that does not leave With.

No, that sentence was doing correction, not relation.

No, you have ignored the context.

No, the speaker used closure, but the sentence did not deserve to close.

No, the grammar says event, but the listener inherited a standing condition.

The objection is not an interruption of the method.

It is part of the method.

A sentence theory that cannot receive objection will become exactly what it was meant to prevent: a forced landing.

The sixth rule is uncertainty.

Do not hide uncertainty.

Mark it.

Some sentences will land cleanly.

Some will not.

Some will admit two readings.

Some will change when context is supplied.

Some will carry a local operation inside a larger social act.

Some will perform one operation for the speaker and another for the listener.

Some will reveal that the registry is not ready.

These sentences are valuable.

They are not failures.

They are where the system becomes honest.

A method becomes trustworthy not by having no difficult cases, but by showing how difficult cases are handled.

So the corpus should contain uncertainty openly.

Perhaps.

Possible.

Context required.

Competing reading.

Forced closure suspected.

Listener objection unresolved.

Operation unclear.

Remainder unstable.

These are not embarrassments.

They are safeguards.

The seventh rule is scale.

A sentence may work locally in one way and socially in another.

The door is closed.

Locally, this may leave a standing condition.

Toward-Is.

But in a conversation, it may be warning, refusal, reassurance, accusation, or closure.

The local sentence-work and the exchange-work may differ.

If I do not mark scale, I may misclassify the sentence.

The sentence as sentence has a landing.

The sentence as used in a situation may have a further act.

The corpus must learn to record both when necessary.

This is hard, but necessary.

Otherwise the registry will flatten living speech into labelled strings.

The eighth rule is return.

Do not classify once and leave.

Return to the sentence later.

A sentence first heard as relation may become correction after the surrounding field is understood.

A sentence first heard as closure may become false closure when objection enters.

A sentence first heard as expansion may become accusation when the speaker's purpose is known.

A sentence first heard as integration may become erasure when the parts that were gathered are found to have been silenced.

Return is not weakness.

Return is fidelity to the field.

The geometry notebook taught this too.

The pair catalogue returned after the longer chord was locked.

The triple catalogue returned after the right angle entered.

The earlier page was not false.

It was incomplete in a disciplined way.

The corpus must allow the same.

First hearing.

Second hearing.

Contextual hearing.

Objection hearing.

Revised hearing.

Only then can the registry begin to deserve its cells.

So I can now write the corpus instruction plainly.

For each sentence, preserve:

the sentence itself,

the minimal field,

the apparent operation,

the apparent orientation,

the apparent remainder,

the reason for the reading,

the possible objection,

the uncertainty,

the scale of use,

and whether the sentence should return later.

This is not yet the registry.

It is the preparation by which a registry may become lawful.

The sentence must not disappear inside the label.

The label must help the sentence become inspectable.

If a label makes the sentence vanish, the label has failed.

The first corpus will therefore look modest.

It will not impress like a table.

It will not announce fifty-four cells.

It will gather sentences and ask them to land under inspection.

That is the work now.

Not completion.

Collection.

Not mastery.

Listening under discipline.

The crossings are waiting.

The sentences must enter first.
