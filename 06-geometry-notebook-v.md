# [Editor’s Note: Ratio Fragment]

This fragment follows the right-angle lock. Its importance lies in its order.

The writer does not allow trigonometric names to arrive first. The names are withheld until the triangle has earned a stable comparison among its sides. The fragment is therefore less a lesson in values than a lesson in disciplined arrival: construction before right angle, right angle before side pattern, side pattern before ratio, ratio before names.

## Geometry Notebook V
### The Names Come After the Ratios

Triangle BFE has landed.

BE is the diameter.

F lies on the circle.

The angle BFE is right.

FE is one preserved opening.

BE is two preserved openings.

BF has been locked as sqrt(3).

So the side pattern is:

1 : sqrt(3) : 2.

This pattern must now be read carefully.

It is not only three lengths standing beside one another. It is the first stable comparison inside the right triangle. The shortest side stands to the hypotenuse as 1 stands to 2. The longer leg stands to the hypotenuse as sqrt(3) stands to 2. The longer leg stands to the shorter leg as sqrt(3) stands to 1.

These are ratios.

A ratio is not a new object in the drawing.

It does not add a side.

It does not add a point.

It does not add a line.

It is the way one established length is held against another established length.

This means ratio must come after length.

And length must come after proof.

And proof must come after construction.

If I say the ratio first, I may still say something true, but I will have made the truth float. It will no longer remember the act that gave it authority.

The triangle teaches a new kind of preservation.

The side lengths may change in a larger similar triangle, but the comparisons may remain.

A triangle with sides

2 : 2sqrt(3) : 4

is not the same size as BFE.

A triangle with sides

3 : 3sqrt(3) : 6

is not the same size either.

But each preserves the same comparisons.

The shortest side remains half the hypotenuse.

The longer leg remains sqrt(3)/2 of the hypotenuse.

The longer leg remains sqrt(3) times the shorter leg.

This is similarity.

Similarity is not sameness of size.

It is sameness of form through preserved ratio.

So another correction enters:

same shape is not same size.

The old law returns in a new place.

Same length was not same work.

Same carrier was not same segment.

Same direction was not same line.

Now same ratio is not same length.

Each time the construction gives me a new sameness, it asks me to name what has survived.

Congruent figures preserve size and shape.

Similar figures preserve shape and ratio, but not necessarily size.

If I confuse these, I will ask the wrong sameness to do the wrong work.

The triangle has therefore opened comparison without destroying difference.

It lets one figure change size while retaining the relations that make it recognizable. The figure may grow. The measurements may change. But the ratio-pattern survives.

This is lawful change again.

Something changes.

Something remains.

The invariant must be named.

Now the familiar names may approach, but only now.

In a right triangle, choose one acute angle.

One side lies opposite that angle.

One side lies beside it.

The longest side lies opposite the right angle. That side is the hypotenuse.

Three comparisons become stable:

opposite to hypotenuse,

adjacent to hypotenuse,

opposite to adjacent.

The names sine, cosine, and tangent may now be used.

Sine names opposite over hypotenuse.

Cosine names adjacent over hypotenuse.

Tangent names opposite over adjacent.

But the names do not create the comparisons.

The comparisons were already there.

The names arrive as receipts.

For the 30-degree angle in BFE, the side opposite is 1, the adjacent side is sqrt(3), and the hypotenuse is 2.

So:

sin 30 degrees = 1/2.

cos 30 degrees = sqrt(3)/2.

tan 30 degrees = 1/sqrt(3).

For the 60-degree angle, the opposite and adjacent sides exchange roles.

So:

sin 60 degrees = sqrt(3)/2.

cos 60 degrees = 1/2.

tan 60 degrees = sqrt(3).

These values are not a table.

They are the memory of the locked triangle.

The names have arrived late enough to be honest.

I must hold to this order because names can become dangerous. A name can make a relation seem possessed before it has been understood. A person may recite sine, cosine, tangent and still not know what act allowed those words to land.

The page does not forbid names.

It disciplines them.

First the right triangle.

Then the side pattern.

Then the comparison.

Then the ratio names.

Now the circle itself can be read differently.

At first, the circle was the swept trace of the held opening around O. Every point on it was one preserved opening from O.

Later, after the right angle and ratios have been earned, a radius leaning from O may be read by parts. If the radius is one, and if a right triangle is drawn from its lean, then one part may be read across and another part may be read upward.

The across-part belongs to cosine.

The up-part belongs to sine.

But this reading must not be allowed to rewrite the beginning.

The circle did not begin as a pair of perpendicular coordinates.

The field did not begin as a square grid.

The radius did not begin as cos and sin.

The unit radius came first.

The right triangle came later.

The ratios came after that.

Only then can a point on the unit circle be read through those names.

Projection is a late reading.

It is not the substrate.

This distinction matters because a later language can become so useful that it pretends to have been first. But a useful reading is still a reading. It must remember what made it possible.

The native construction began with a held opening, a circle, six stations, seven points, joins, triples, diameter, right angle, and ratio.

Only after that can projection speak honestly.

The names come after the ratios.

The ratios come after the triangle.

The triangle comes after the right angle.

The right angle comes after the diameter.

The diameter comes after the six stations.

The six stations come after the circle.

The circle comes after the held opening.

The held opening comes after the first refusal:

do not change what must be preserved.

That refusal is still underneath the names.

Even when the page says sine.

Even when it says cosine.

Even when it says tangent.

The oldest law is still present:

something may move,

something may change role,

something may grow,

something may be renamed,

but the preserved relation must be accounted for.

This is why a formula is not enough.

A formula can be correct and still arrive too early.

A name can be exact and still be unearned.

A value can be true and still be used without the route that makes it shareable.

The right triangle has taught the ratios.

The ratios have received names.

The names must remain grateful.
