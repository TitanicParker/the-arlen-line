# [Editor’s Note: Native Coordinate Fragment]

This fragment follows the ratio passage in the same geometry notebook. The transition is significant.

The writer does not introduce coordinates as an external grid. Coordinates appear only after the construction has produced centre, unit steps, repeated directions, parallel families, length classes, ratio, and field pressure. Position now has to be remembered. The coordinate is therefore not a label pasted onto a point. It is the compressed memory of a lawful route.

## Geometry Notebook VI
### Position Needs Memory

The figure has begun to exceed sight.

At first, I could look at the seven points and hold them together.

O.

A, B, C, D, E, F.

Then the joins came.

Then the triples.

Then the right angle.

Then the waiting length.

Then ratio.

Each new relation made the page more exact, but also harder to keep only by looking. The eye can see a point, but sight alone does not remember how the point was reached. If the field continues, I need another kind of memory.

This is where coordinates begin.

Not as a square grid.

Not as lines brought from outside.

Not as numbers pasted onto the page.

A coordinate is needed because a lawful point must be findable again by another person.

The construction already has directions. It has unit steps. It has repeated carriers. It has a centre. It has the pressure of a field.

Choose two neighbouring unit directions from the field.

Call them u and v.

They are not perpendicular. They meet at sixty degrees because they belong to the triangular construction. Each has length one because each carries the preserved opening.

A point may now be reached by taking some number of steps in direction u and some number of steps in direction v.

Let the number of u-steps be a.

Let the number of v-steps be b.

Then the point may be recorded as:

(a, b)

But this pair is not the point.

It is the memory of how the point was reached.

The coordinate says:

go this many lawful steps one way,

and this many lawful steps in the neighbouring way.

If reverse travel is allowed, the numbers may be negative. A step can be undone. A direction can be followed backward. The field is not trapped in one wedge.

So a and b may be integers.

The coordinate field opens.

But I must be careful again.

The route to a point is not the same as the straight distance from O.

A point may be reached by two field steps and still not be two units away in a straight line.

This is the first correction of coordinates:

path is not span.

A path remembers how I travelled.

A span measures the straight separation.

Both matter.

Neither may replace the other.

Suppose the point is reached by one step in direction u and one step in direction v.

Its coordinate is:

(1, 1)

The route took two unit steps.

But the two steps lean toward each other at sixty degrees. They do not make a straight line of length two. The straight span from O is shorter than two.

To find the straight distance, the field must write its own rule.

Let u be read across the page as one unit.

Let v lean at sixty degrees.

Then one step of v contributes half a unit across and sqrt(3)/2 upward.

So a steps in u and b steps in v give:

across part: a + b/2

upward part: b sqrt(3)/2

The squared straight distance from O is therefore:

(a + b/2)^2 + (b sqrt(3)/2)^2

Expand it:

a^2 + ab + b^2/4 + 3b^2/4

The b-parts combine:

a^2 + ab + b^2

So the native squared-distance rule is:

a^2 + ab + b^2

The actual straight distance is:

sqrt(a^2 + ab + b^2)

The middle term matters.

ab is not clutter.

ab is not a mistake.

ab remembers that the two native directions meet at sixty degrees. If the directions were perpendicular, that term would not belong here. But this is not a square grid. The field must not be made to forget its own angle.

The algebra remembers the geometry.

Test the rule.

For (1, 0):

1^2 + 1*0 + 0^2 = 1.

The distance is 1.

For (0, 1):

0^2 + 0*1 + 1^2 = 1.

The distance is 1.

For (1, 1):

1^2 + 1*1 + 1^2 = 3.

The distance is sqrt(3).

For (2, 0):

2^2 + 2*0 + 0^2 = 4.

The distance is 2.

The old lengths return.

1.

sqrt(3).

2.

They are not invented by the formula. They were already earned by the construction. The formula lets them be searched beyond the first drawing.

This is another kind of carrying.

The compass carried length through motion.

The sentence carried instruction through attention.

The coordinate carries route through symbol.

The formula carries distance through memory.

But each must remember what it carries.

A coordinate is not a point.

A formula is not the field.

A number is not the act.

The coordinate compresses the route.

The formula remembers the span.

The construction gives both their authority.

Now consider the direction halfway between u and v.

In the sector between those directions, the points with equal coordinates lie along the bisecting ray.

a = b.

The first such point is:

(1, 1)

It takes two field steps to reach:

one step in u,

one step in v.

But its straight distance is sqrt(3), not 2.

Again, path is not span.

The ray is lawful in the field, but it is not a nearest-neighbour edge direction. It is carried through field points. It appears because recurrence allows the field to show more directions than the first six unit edges.

This is important.

The field is not an equal-angle machine.

The first ring gives six unit directions.

The second ring reveals directions between them.

Later points may reveal other exact carried directions.

But exact does not mean evenly spaced.

A direction may be lawful because points carry it, not because the circle has been divided into equal angular pieces.

The page must not become lazy here.

Ring number is not straight distance.

Coordinate route is not straight distance.

Direction carried by points is not the same as nearest-neighbour direction.

A square-grid reader may want to flatten all of this. The reader may want coordinates to mean the familiar thing. But the construction did not begin with a square. It began with a held opening, a circle, six stations, and a triangular field pressing outward.

The coordinate language must be faithful to that beginning.

A coordinate records movement history in the native field.

The squared-distance rule records straight separation.

The square root gives actual distance.

The middle term remembers the sixty-degree relation.

The old lengths return inside algebra.

The equal-coordinate ray gives a carried thirty-degree direction in this sector.

And none of this replaces the construction.

This is why I trust coordinates only after the field has demanded them.

If coordinates arrive too early, they rule the page before the page has spoken. They make the point look like a place in a pre-existing system. But here the point is not born inside a grid. It is reached by acts the construction has earned.

The symbol comes late.

The route comes first.

This may also be true of words.

A word can name a thing after the relation has been found. But if the word arrives too early, it can make the relation seem settled before anyone has walked it.

The coordinate is a good word only when it remembers the path.

The formula is a good sentence only when it remembers the field.

So I must keep writing the order:

preserved opening,

circle,

stations,

joins,

triples,

right angle,

ratio,

repeated directions,

coordinate pressure,

route-memory,

native distance rule.

Only then may the point be written as a pair.

Only then may the pair be trusted.
