# [Editor’s Note: Transformation Fragment]

This fragment follows the cell and area passage. It appears to close the first long movement of the geometry notebook.

The writer has now passed through point, opening, circle, stations, joins, triples, right angle, ratio, coordinates, recurrence, cells, and area. Here those earlier discoveries are gathered under a single discipline: lawful change. A thing may move, turn, reverse, scale, or be rearranged only when the relation preserved by that change has been named.

## Geometry Notebook IX
### What Change Is Allowed to Keep

The construction began with change.

The hand moved.

The compass turned.

The pencil travelled.

The curve appeared.

But the opening did not change.

That was the first rule.

Move, but do not alter this distance.

At first I thought of that as a tool instruction. Now it seems older than the tool. It is the first form of lawful change. Something is permitted to move because something else is not permitted to drift.

This may be what an invariant is.

Not a dead thing.

Not a label.

Not a number written beside a figure.

An invariant is what the change is not allowed to destroy.

The compass could move because the opening was held.

Now the figure can change in other ways.

It can rotate.

It can reflect.

It can translate.

It can scale.

It can be cut and recomposed.

But each change must say what remains.

Rotate the seven-point residue by one sixth-turn around O.

A moves to B.

B moves to C.

C moves to D.

D moves to E.

E moves to F.

F returns to A.

The names change position. The figure turns. But the distances remain. The angles remain. Ranges of neighbour and opposite remain. Raddii remain radii. Unit chords remain unit chords. Diameters remain diameters. The central equilateral triangles remain central equilateral triangles.

Rotation permits turning.

It preserves distance and angle.

That is its honesty.

Now reflect the figure across the diameter AD.

A and D remain fixed.

B and F exchange.

C and E exchange.

The figure reverses handedness.

A triangle that leaned one way now leans the other way. But its side lengths do not change. Its angle sizes do not change. Its family does not disappear.

Reflection permits reversal.

It preserves distance and angle while changing handedness.

That is its honesty.

Now translate a triangle through the field.

Do not turn it.

Do not stretch it.

Carry it to another lawful station.

If the field has recurrence, the same local relation can appear elsewhere. A unit edge can move to another unit edge. An equilateral cell can appear in another place. A direction can be carried without rotation.

Translation permits relocation.

It preserves local distance, angle, direction, and shape.

That is its honesty.

Now scale the right triangle.

Let 1 : sqrt(3) : 2 become:

2 : 2sqrt(3) : 4

or

3 : 3sqrt(3) : 6

or

10 : 10sqrt(3) : 20.

The lengths have changed.

The size has changed.

But the ratios remain. The angle pattern remains. The shape remains in the sense of similarity, though not in the sense of congruence.

Scaling permits size-change.

It preserves angle and ratio.

That is its honesty.

Now cut a figure into cells and move the pieces.

This is more dangerous.

A whole figure may be broken. Its outline may disappear. Its pieces may be turned, shifted, and rearranged. The final body may look different from the first body.

Recomposition permits rearrangement.

It preserves area only if the pieces are kept.

No stretching.

No loss.

No duplication.

No hidden overlap.

No unfilled gap.

That is its honesty.

Each transformation has its own law.

Rotation is not reflection.

Reflection is not translation.

Translation is not scaling.

Scaling is not recomposition.

The word change is too blunt.

The question is:

what kind of change?

And then:

what has survived?

If distance survives, say distance.

If angle survives, say angle.

If direction survives, say direction.

If ratio survives, say ratio.

If area survives, say area.

If handedness changes, say it changed.

If size changes, say it changed.

If outline changes, say it changed.

Do not let one invariant pretend to be another.

Same area is not same shape.

Same shape is not same size.

Same size is not same position.

Same direction is not same carrier.

Same carrier is not same segment.

Same length is not same work.

The page has been teaching this from the beginning.

Every time I thought sameness had been settled, the construction asked:

same in what way?

Now I can see why.

A false completion often hides inside an unnamed sameness.

A person says two things are the same.

Which same?

Same appearance?

Same measure?

Same role?

Same route?

Same family?

Same effect?

Same authority?

Same surface?

Same conclusion?

Without the preserved relation named, the sentence may close too quickly.

Geometry is strict because it makes the mistake visible.

If I rotate a figure and claim that position has remained, the page refuses me.

If I scale a figure and claim that length has remained, the page refuses me.

If I recompose pieces and claim shape has remained, the page refuses me.

If I reflect a figure and pretend handedness has not changed, the page refuses me.

The page permits change, but not confusion about change.

This is mercy.

It lets a thing become other than it was without losing what it has the right to keep.

It also refuses to let a thing claim survival where survival has not occurred.

The first compass rule was already saying this.

The hand may move.

The opening may not drift.

Later the same command becomes:

Turn, but keep distance and angle.

Reflect, but admit reversal.

Translate, but do not rotate.

Scale, but keep ratio and admit changed size.

Recompose, but keep the pieces and admit changed outline.

Every lawful transformation is a sentence with a refusal inside it.

It says:

this may change,

this must remain,

this must not be claimed.

I think this is why the geometry feels like speech before speech.

A construction is not only a picture. It is an act that tells the viewer what has been allowed and what has been forbidden. It changes the field of attention. It leaves a remainder. If another person follows the change and sees what remained, the act has become shareable.

Maybe a sentence should be judged in the same way.

Not only by what it says.

By what it changes.

By what it preserves.

By what it eliminates.

By what it refuses to imply.

By what remains after it lands.

But I must not rush there yet.

This page belongs to geometry.

Here is what has been earned:

the fixed opening,

the first circle,

the six boundary stations,

the seven-point residue,

the pair catalogue,

the triple catalogue,

the right angle,

the length sqrt(3),

the ratio pattern,

native coordinates,

recurrence,

rings,

cells,

area,

and now transformation.

All of them return to the same discipline:

something changes,

something remains,

and the invariant must be named.

If the invariant is unnamed, the change is not yet trustworthy.

If the change is not trustworthy, the result has not landed.

If the result has not landed, another mind cannot stand there with me.

So the notebook closes this first movement with a rule that is also a warning:

Do not ask another person to inherit a change unless you can say what survived it.
