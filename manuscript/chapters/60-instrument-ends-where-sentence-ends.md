## Formal Note LI
### The Instrument Ends Where the Sentence Ends

The instrument must know where to stop.

It begins with the sentence and should end where the sentence ends. It may clarify the landing. It may name the structure. It may test the route under rereading. It may receive objection and repair the derivation. But it should not continue past its office as if every question that follows belongs to it.

This limit is difficult because the instrument opens so many doors.

Once a sentence has been walked, the reader may want to ask what it means, whether it is true, what it implies, who benefits, who is harmed, what history surrounds it, what response it requires. These questions are real. They may be urgent. But they are not all sentence-termination questions.

The instrument ends where its object ends.

A single declarative sentence is the object. The landing of that sentence is the target. When the landing has been lawfully named and the route remains returnable, the instrument has done its work. It should not pretend that the next moral, legal, clinical, literary, or political question is already solved by the invariant.

This limit protects the instrument from overreach.

It also protects the next inquiry from being flattened.

If the instrument says too much, it becomes doctrine. If it stops too soon, it may seem evasive. The correct stopping point is not silence, but handoff. The instrument may say: this sentence lands here; the next question belongs to evidence, ethics, law, memory, or interpretation.

This is not failure.

It is office.

A good office knows what it may decide.

The corpus-governance rule returns: everything remains, but not everything rules. The sentence instrument may remain in later inquiry. It may support the analysis. It may show how finality was performed. It may reveal a hidden relation, a correction, a closure, a reversal, or a false state. But it should not rule questions beyond its scope.

This is especially important for institutional material. A sentence may perform premature closure, but the full claim of institutional failure requires evidence beyond that sentence. The instrument can reveal the grammar of foreclosure. It cannot by itself prove the entire case. It must hand off to source hierarchy, chronology, statutory context, correspondence, and record.

The same is true in generated text. A model sentence may overclaim. The instrument can show that it asserts settled fact, necessary implication, or generalized production without licensed source. But the remedy belongs to editorial governance: revise, demote, cite, query, or remove. The invariant identifies the act; the editor decides office.

The same is true in fairy tales. The instrument may name the invariant of a sentence. It does not exhaust the tale’s beauty. It hands the reader back to the story with sharper hearing.

Stopping at the proper boundary is a form of respect.

It respects the sentence by not replacing it.

It respects the reader by not dominating every response.

It respects other disciplines by not pretending to be them.

It respects the instrument by keeping it finite.

The DTI made this limit explicit in formal terms: single declarative sentences, structural termination, finite geometry, collapse conditions. The manuscript must make the same limit felt in human terms. The instrument is powerful because it stops.

A sentence lands.

The instrument names the landing.

The reader returns to the world.

That return is the point.

If the reader remains trapped inside the instrument, the instrument has failed socially even if it succeeded technically. It has converted attention into enclosure. Arlen’s work was never meant to enclose attention. It was meant to make shared reality more possible.

The final discipline of the instrument is therefore release.

After the sentence has landed, let it go.

Let the reader go with it.

Let the next inquiry begin under its own law.
