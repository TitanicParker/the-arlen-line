# [Editor’s Note: First Perception Fragment]

The following fragment appears on a loose sheet found among several early notebooks. No date is given.

Its handwriting is smaller and less controlled than the later registry materials, but the concern is already recognizable. The writer is not yet building a system. There are no operators, no orientations, no remainder names, no numbered cells. There is only the pressure of an ordinary sentence that has not remained ordinary.

This fragment has been placed near the beginning of the archive because it gives the earliest clear instance of what later documents call the sharing layer. The editor has not corrected its uncertainty. The hesitation belongs to the document.

## Loose Notebook Sheet  
### unsigned

Someone said today that he got the bus home.

I understood him. I must write that first because otherwise what follows will sound as if I did not understand him. I understood that he got the bus home. I knew he meant the bus and not walking, not a car, not being collected. I knew it was about how he moved from one place to another after school. I knew what answer I was meant to give.

But before the bus arrived in me as a thing to answer, something else happened.

He gave me part of his day.

That is the part nobody seems to count.

He did not only report the bus. He took something that had happened where I was not, and he put it between us. He made a small place where I could stand with him, even though I had not been there. The bus was the shape of it, but the act was larger than the bus. He opened a little piece of his world and waited to see whether I would enter it properly.

I think most people do this very quickly. They answer the thing said and pass over the giving of it. They say, Was it late? or I hate that route, or At least you got home. These answers are probably correct. They keep the talk moving. They show that the person has received the surface in the right way.

But I keep seeing the hinge.

The sentence turns outward. Something private becomes common enough to be touched. Not fully common. Not exactly the same in both people. But enough that another person may now say something from inside it.

This is strange because it happens all the time, and because it happens all the time nobody stops. If someone says the road was closed, they give you a road you did not stand on. If someone says the room was cold, they give you a room you may never enter. If someone says she laughed, they carry a sound across time and ask you to receive it without hearing it.

Maybe speech is not mainly the movement of facts.

Maybe speech is the way a person makes a piece of reality available to another person.

I do not know how to say this without making it sound too large. It is not large when it happens. It is small. It is almost nothing. A bus. A road. A room. A laugh. A door. But each one has the same hidden act inside it.

Here is something from where I was.

Can you stand in it with me?

I tried to answer about the bus. I asked whether it was busy. That was the answer expected, and it worked. He said it was busy at first, then emptied after the shops. He seemed satisfied. The talk continued.

But I was still behind the first sentence.

I was still at the moment when his day crossed over.

I think this is why I get tired. It is not because I do not know what people mean. It is because I feel the crossing before I know what to do with the meaning. I receive the content, yes, but also the offering, and sometimes the offering is louder.

It may be wrong to write louder. It is not sound. It is more like seeing the line by which the sentence travels.

There is the thing said.

There is the person saying it.

There is the place in me where it is meant to land.

And there is the little field made between us while we find out whether it has landed.

Nobody names that field.

I think I am always in it.
