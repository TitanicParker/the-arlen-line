# [Editor’s Note: First Protocol Fragment]

The following note begins the practical use of the registry.

The writer has now refused premature completion several times. The six rows have been opened. Each row has been disturbed by objection. The registry has been defined as a return instrument rather than a verdict machine. The next question is practical: how is the instrument used on an actual sentence?

This fragment is the first answer. It is deliberately procedural, but not mechanical. The writer wants a route another mind can walk, not a checklist that replaces listening.

## Formal Note XXV
### The First Use Protocol

The registry now asks for method.

A person cannot simply look at a sentence and name a cell.

That would be too quick.

A sentence must be entered.

The field must be approached.

The before must be heard.

The after must be tested.

The route must be shown.

So I will write the first protocol.

Not final.

First.

Protocol means a disciplined way of entering a sentence so that another mind may inspect the reading.

The first step is preservation.

Write the sentence as it was given.

Do not improve it.

Do not clean it.

Do not replace its ordinary words with clearer ones.

Do not remove hesitation, small words, tense, pronouns, or awkwardness too early.

The sentence itself must remain.

If the sentence is:

It was only a joke.

Do not rewrite it as:

The speaker minimized the seriousness of the act.

That may be a later interpretation.

It is not the sentence.

The original wording carries the landing.

Only.

Was.

Joke.

Each word matters.

The second step is minimal field.

What is the smallest field needed for the sentence to be inspectable?

Who speaks?

To whom?

After what?

In answer to what?

What is already open?

What does the sentence seem to change?

Do not invent a full story if only a small field is known.

But do not pretend there is no field.

A bare sentence is useful for training.

A fielded sentence is necessary for judgment.

The third step is before-field.

What could the listener hold before the sentence?

Not everything.

Only the relevant openness.

Was the location unknown?

Was the question unsettled?

Was a relation unclear?

Was a claim under test?

Was a whole fragmented?

Was a process still open?

The before-field is X.

The sentence takes X.

If X is unknown, mark it.

Do not hide it.

The fourth step is after-field.

What does the sentence ask the listener to hold after it lands?

What remains?

A condition?

A withness?

An event?

Something standing?

Something held together?

Something done or happening?

This is Y.

The sentence takes X and leaves Y.

If Y is disputed, mark it.

The fifth step is operation.

What work changes X into Y?

Distinction?

Expansion?

Relation?

Correction?

Integration?

Closure?

Do not choose by topic.

Choose by work.

Ask:

Did the sentence make difference answerable?

Did it make reach answerable?

Did it make withness answerable?

Did it change a prior inadequacy toward answerability?

Did it gather plurality into organized participation?

Did it bring a field to rest, settlement, cessation, or completion?

If more than one operation appears, mark the scale.

Local sentence-work.

Field-work.

Exchange-work.

Ethical pressure.

The sixth step is orientation.

Which way did the operation move?

Toward.

Across.

Away.

Toward gathers toward a point, condition, result, relation, act, or settlement.

Across holds through span, cases, time, distribution, comparison, recurrence, or process.

Away departs from, releases, withdraws, separates from, moves beyond, or leaves a prior condition.

The orientation is not decoration.

It tells how the operation travels.

The seventh step is remainder.

What form does the sentence leave?

Is.

With.

Does.

Is leaves standing condition.

With leaves relation, participation, opposition, or held-togetherness.

Does leaves event, act, occurrence, process, or happening.

The remainder is not Closure.

Closure is an operator.

Is, With, Does are forms of what remains.

This distinction must be kept.

The eighth step is cell proposal.

Only now may the cell be named.

Operation-orientation-remainder.

Distinction-Toward-Is.

Expansion-Across-Does.

Relation-Away-With.

Correction-Toward-Does.

Integration-Across-With.

Closure-Away-Is.

The cell proposal is not a verdict.

It is a route-name.

It says:

Here is where I think the sentence crossed.

Now inspect the path.

The ninth step is reason.

Give the reason in ordinary language.

Do not merely repeat the cell name.

Say what changed.

Say why the operation leads.

Say why the orientation fits.

Say why the remainder is left.

A reason should allow another mind to agree or disagree.

Bad reason:

It is Closure because it closes.

Better reason:

The sentence treats the matter as no longer open, moves the listener toward settled condition, and leaves the case standing as closed.

A reason must show the route.

The tenth step is objection.

What could challenge the reading?

Another operation?

Another orientation?

Another remainder?

Missing context?

Listener refusal?

Ethical problem?

Scale confusion?

False claim?

Premature closure?

Forced relation?

Overreach?

Erasure?

Control?

Objection must enter before confidence.

Not after.

The eleventh step is uncertainty.

Mark uncertainty openly.

Low.

Medium.

High.

Context required.

Competing reading.

Listener objection unresolved.

Ethical status unresolved.

Local reading only.

Scale unclear.

The registry becomes more trustworthy when it admits what it does not yet know.

The twelfth step is return.

Should this sentence be returned to later?

Some sentences are clean training examples.

Some are resistant examples.

Some are key boundary cases.

Some require more context.

Some expose a weakness in the row.

Mark return.

No return needed.

Return for scale.

Return for ethics.

Return for operator boundary.

Return after listener account.

Return after corpus comparison.

The thirteenth step is use.

Why is the reading being made?

Training?

Repair?

Argument?

Editorial decision?

Teaching?

Self-audit?

Corpus entry?

Ethical inspection?

The same sentence may be treated differently depending on use. The protocol should know what it is being asked to do.

The fourteenth step is restraint.

Do not say more than the protocol has earned.

If the field is thin, say provisional.

If the listener landing is unknown, say unknown.

If ethical status is unresolved, do not resolve it by classification.

If the route cannot be shown, do not trust the cell.

Now I can write the protocol in shorter form.

Preserve the sentence.

Give the minimal field.

Name the before-field.

Name the after-field.

Identify the operation.

Identify the orientation.

Identify the remainder.

Propose the cell.

Give the reason.

Invite objection.

Mark uncertainty.

Mark return.

State use.

Practice restraint.

This is the first use protocol.

It is not meant to make reading fast.

It is meant to make reading shareable.

A quick label belongs to possession.

A shown route belongs to shared inspection.

The protocol should slow the mind down until the sentence can be walked.

This is the discipline.

Not to know immediately.

To make knowing answerable.
