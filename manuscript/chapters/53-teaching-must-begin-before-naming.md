## Formal Note XLIV
### Teaching Must Begin Before Naming

The first lesson should not name the registry.

This is difficult because names feel like progress. Once a thing has a name, it appears to have been grasped. Simple Placement, Boundary Imposition, Directional Pivot, Structural Restoration, Complete Cessation: the names are strong. They give the mind handles. They make recurrence visible.

But a handle offered too soon becomes a substitute for touch.

Teaching must begin before naming.

The learner must first encounter the act without the label. A sentence is read. It lands. Another sentence is read. It does not land. A third sentence lands differently from the first. The learner is asked to notice the difference before being told what to call it.

This is not a pedagogical trick. It preserves the order of reality.

The landing precedes the name.

The name is lawful only if it retrieves the landing.

If naming comes first, the learner begins to hunt for labels. The sentence becomes prey. The registry becomes a net. The learner asks, “Which invariant is this?” before asking, “What happened when the sentence came to rest?” That order trains classification without perception.

Arlen’s work requires the opposite.

Perception first.

Naming second.

Registry third.

Instrument fourth.

This order should govern any public teaching sequence. Begin with pairs, not tables.

“The door closed.”

“The door closed because...”

Let the learner feel completion and noncompletion.

Then:

“The door closed.”

“The door is closed.”

Let the learner feel event and state.

Then:

“The rule applies here.”

“The rule applies across all cases.”

Let the learner feel convergence and span.

Then:

“The machine stopped.”

“The machine stopped malfunctioning.”

Let the learner feel termination and correction.

Only after such differences have begun to register should the names be introduced. Even then, they should be introduced as handles for already-felt distinctions, not as categories imposed from above.

This is how the fairy-tale corpus teaches when used properly. It does not begin by arguing for Singular Selection. It lets “I will not” recur until the refusal begins to feel like a structural act. It does not define Directional Pivot before the hen says, “Then I will.” It lets the turn be felt. It does not explain Final Termination before “So she did.” It lets the act end.

Teaching by recurrence is slower than teaching by list.

It is also more faithful.

A list can be memorised. A recurrence can be recognised. Memorisation can operate without return. Recognition can be called back to the sentence. The teacher must therefore resist the classroom desire for efficient naming. Efficiency here can damage the very faculty being trained.

This does not mean the names are unimportant.

They are necessary. Without names, recognition remains private. A learner may feel the difference but be unable to share it, dispute it, or compare it. The name gives social form to the landing. It lets two readers return to the same act and ask whether the same invariant has been seen.

But the name must remain answerable.

If a learner says “Directional Pivot,” the teacher should ask: where did the direction change? What prior course was altered? Why is this not merely sequence? Why is this not Closure? What survives rereading?

The question is not meant to embarrass. It restores the name to its function. It teaches that a name is not a badge of mastery. It is a retrieval handle. If it does not retrieve the sentence’s act, it is noise.

This point should be made explicitly in the public instrument.

The names are not meanings.

The names are not morals.

The names are not themes.

The names are not literary interpretations.

The names are not psychological states.

The names are names for structural landings.

Even this formulation is slightly incomplete. More exactly: they are names for the crossing of a dominant transformation with a geometry of rest. But that phrase belongs later. The first teaching must not ask the learner to carry it. The learner begins with noticing.

This order also protects against over-poetic use. Many invariant names are attractive. Retrospective Reinterpretation, Harmonic Equilibrium, Relational Emergence, Complete Cessation: these can be used ornamentally. A reader may want to apply them because they sound right. Teaching before naming prevents that drift. The learner must show the sentence before enjoying the name.

The sentence disciplines the vocabulary.

A teaching sequence faithful to the archive might therefore proceed in five movements.

First, landing and nonlanding.

Second, the three forms of rest: Is, With, Does.

Third, the three directions of attention: Toward, Across, Away.

Fourth, the six operators as transformations required for rest.

Fifth, the registry as the computed crossing of operator and ending.

The registry comes last because it is the receipt of prior distinctions.

Placed first, it becomes doctrine.

Placed last, it becomes memory.

This is also how the reader is protected from the scale of the system. Fifty-four feels large when encountered as a table. It feels smaller when the reader already understands that it is only six transformations crossed with nine endings. The nine feel smaller when the reader understands they are three forms of rest crossed with three directions of attention. The three feel smaller when the reader has already felt a sentence land.

The system becomes teachable by returning to the smallest act.

That is the public pedagogy.

Not simplification by dilution.

Simplification by lawful order.
