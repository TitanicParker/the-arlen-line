# [Editor’s Note: Registry Use Fragment]

The following note appears to define the proper use of the registry.

The writer is concerned that a reader might treat the registry as a classificatory machine. This fragment corrects that. The registry is not a device for quickly assigning labels. It is a return instrument. Its purpose is to send the reader back into the sentence more carefully than before.

This fragment is important because it prepares the later protocol. The registry becomes useful only when it is used as route, not verdict.

## Formal Note XXIV
### The Registry Is a Return Instrument

The registry must not be used as a verdict machine.

This sentence is Distinction-Toward-Is.

This sentence is Relation-Across-With.

This sentence is Closure-Away-Does.

These statements may be useful.

They may also become dead very quickly.

If the cell name ends the work, the registry has failed.

A cell name should begin return.

It should make the sentence more inspectable, not less.

So the first use of the registry is not classification.

It is return.

Return to the sentence.

Return to the field.

Return to the before.

Return to the after.

Return to the listener.

Return to the objection.

Return to the route by which the sentence changed what could be shared.

The registry is a return instrument.

It asks:

What did you hear?

Where did it move?

What did it leave?

Why do you think so?

Can another mind walk that route?

This is different from labelling.

A label can be placed quickly.

A route must be shown.

The difference matters.

If I say:

The case is settled is Closure-Toward-Is.

I have named a possible crossing.

But the responsible use of the registry asks:

What was open before the sentence?

Who says it is settled?

What process settled it?

Who could object?

What remains standing?

Is the closure local or total?

Does the sentence give rest or refuse answer?

Only then does the cell name become useful.

It gathers questions.

It does not end them.

If I say:

The rumor spread through the school is Expansion-Across-Does.

I have named reach across a field as event.

But the registry must return me:

How far did it spread?

What evidence shows the range?

What changed as it moved?

Was the spread accidental?

Was it harmful?

Did the sentence merely report expansion, or did it amplify it by repeating it?

This last question is important.

A sentence about expansion may itself expand what it names.

Saying the rumor spread may spread the rumor further.

A sentence may report an operation and perform another.

The registry must be alert to this.

If I say:

I know exactly how you feel is Relation-Toward-With.

The cell name is not enough.

The return asks:

Was withness offered?

Was it earned?

Did the listener receive comfort or erasure?

Did the sentence preserve difference?

Did it bind too quickly?

Could the listener refuse the relation?

The cell name returns me to the human field.

That is its purpose.

The second use of the registry is comparison.

Two sentences may share a subject but differ in operation.

The door is closed.

The door closed behind him.

Close the door.

The door keeps them apart.

The door finally shut out the noise.

The subject is door.

But the work differs.

Standing condition.

Event.

Command.

Separation.

Closure with relief.

The registry helps the reader stop mistaking subject for operation.

It also helps compare similar operations with different landings.

The door is closed.

Closure-Toward-Is or perhaps Distinction-Toward-Is, depending on field.

The door closed behind him.

Closure-Away-Does.

The closed door separated them.

Distinction-Away-With or Closure-Away-With, depending on work.

The difference is not ornamental.

It changes what the listener inherits.

The third use of the registry is disagreement.

Two readers may hear different crossings.

This should not be treated as failure too quickly.

One reader hears Correction.

Another hears Closure.

One hears Relation.

Another hears forced Integration.

One hears Distinction.

Another hears exclusion.

The registry gives them a way to compare routes.

It asks each reader:

What before-field are you assuming?

What change do you hear?

What remains for you?

Where does the sentence move?

What objection do you bring?

The disagreement may show that one reader missed the context.

It may show that the sentence is doing work at two scales.

It may show that the listener's field differs from the speaker's.

It may show that the sentence is ambiguous.

It may show that the registry needs refinement.

This is good.

A shareable instrument should make disagreement more exact.

It should not pretend disagreement has vanished.

The fourth use is repair.

If a sentence lands badly, the registry may help locate the damage.

A speaker says:

I was only explaining the difference.

The listener received exclusion.

The registry can ask:

Was the local operation Distinction?

Was the exchange-work Closure?

Was the ethical pressure exclusion?

What boundary was drawn?

What relation was cut?

What could be said to reopen the field?

Repair then becomes more exact.

Not:

I did not mean it.

But:

I can see that I drew the boundary there.

I can see that it landed as exclusion.

I meant to distinguish the cases, but I closed your place in the conversation.

Let me return to the field.

This is how the registry may serve mercy.

It does not replace apology.

It makes apology more answerable.

The fifth use is teaching.

A student may learn that sentences are not only containers of content.

They do things.

They change the field.

They leave remainders.

A sentence may seem simple and still perform a complex operation.

The registry can train attention.

Not to make the student mechanical.

To make the student careful.

The question becomes:

What did the sentence ask another mind to hold?

This is perhaps the simplest teaching form.

What did it ask another mind to hold?

A difference?

A reach?

A relation?

A repair?

A whole?

An ending?

And in what form did it leave that holding?

As Is?

As With?

As Does?

The sixth use is self-audit.

Before I speak, I may ask:

What am I about to do to the shared field?

Am I drawing a distinction?

Am I expanding the scope?

Am I binding terms?

Am I correcting?

Am I gathering parts?

Am I closing?

Is that the right operation?

Have I earned it?

Will the listener be able to inspect the route?

Am I using closure because I am tired?

Am I using correction because I want control?

Am I using relation because I fear distance?

Am I using integration because I want disagreement to disappear?

Am I using expansion because the specific truth is uncomfortable?

Am I using distinction because relation would cost me something?

This is not the registry as theory.

This is the registry as conscience.

The seventh use is editorial.

The present book must use the registry on itself.

When the editor joins fragments, is that Integration or forced Integration?

When a note is clarified, is that Correction or control?

When a sequence is closed, is that lawful Closure or premature book-shape?

When a term is distinguished, is the distinction earned?

When a concept is expanded, does the expansion remain answerable?

When two source fragments are related, is the relation supported?

The registry must inspect the book that carries it.

Otherwise the book would teach a law it does not obey.

This may be the deepest use.

A method that cannot inspect its own making should not govern speech.

Now I can state the registry use law:

Do not use the registry to finish the sentence.

Use it to return to the sentence.

Do not use the cell to silence objection.

Use it to make objection exact.

Do not use the row to replace listening.

Use it to train listening.

Do not use classification as authority.

Use classification as route.

Do not use the table to dominate the field.

Use the table to make the field shareable.

The registry becomes lawful only when another person can walk the path back from the name to the sentence.

If they cannot, the name has become decoration.

If they can, the name may help.

This is enough for the present stage.

The six rows have been seen together.

The registry has been defined as return.

The next work cannot be more naming.

It must become protocol.

How does one actually use the registry on a sentence?

What steps must be taken?

What must be preserved?

Where does objection enter?

Where does uncertainty remain?

Where does repair begin?

The registry now asks for method.
