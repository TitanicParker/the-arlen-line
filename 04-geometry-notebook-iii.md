# [Editor’s Note: Triple Catalogue Fragment]

This fragment continues the same geometry notebook. It appears after the pair catalogue and seems to answer a problem left by that earlier passage: if two points open a join, what does the selection of three points open?

The fragment is important because it refuses the easy answer. Three selected points do not automatically become a triangle. They must be inspected. Some selections enclose a body. Some collapse into straightness. Some belong to families that cannot yet be fully spent.

The editor has retained the manuscript's count language because count is not being used decoratively here. Count functions as a guard against vague examples.

## Geometry Notebook III
### Three Points Do Not Yet Make a Triangle

The pair catalogue is not the end.

A pair gives a join.

Now choose three points.

I want to say that three points make a triangle, but the construction will not allow that sentence yet.

There are seven points:

O, A, B, C, D, E, F.

If I choose three of them, the number of possible selections is thirty-five. This number does not say there are thirty-five triangles. It says there are thirty-five triple selections. A selection is only the beginning of inspection.

Some triples fail to enclose a body.

O, A, and D lie on one straight carrier.

O, B, and E lie on another.

O, C, and F lie on another.

These are still triples. They are not mistakes. But they do not form triangles. They collapse into straight relations. They leave straight angles: AOD, BOE, COF.

So the first rule of this page is:

three selected points do not automatically make a triangle.

The page has corrected the sentence before I could finish it.

A pair always opens a join.

A triple opens a test.

Does it enclose a body?

Does it collapse into straightness?

Does it carry the centre?

Does it belong only to the boundary?

What family does it enter?

What must wait?

There are thirty-five triples in all.

Three collapse.

Thirty-two remain as genuine triangles.

This subtraction matters. The three failed triangles are not discarded because they are useless. They teach straightness. They teach that selection may leave a remainder other than the one expected. They teach that refusal belongs inside the catalogue.

The false triangle does not vanish.

It becomes a straight-angle relation.

Now inspect the triples that use O.

To make a triple with O, choose any two of the six boundary stations.

There are fifteen such choices.

Three have already collapsed: OAD, OBE, OCF.

Twelve remain as genuine centre-boundary triangles.

Six of these are already known:

OAB,

OBC,

OCD,

ODE,

OEF,

OFA.

These are the central equilateral triangles. Each has two radii and one neighbouring chord. Each side carries the preserved opening. They do not need to be guessed again. The earlier proof has returned as catalogue.

This is another thing I must remember:

a known fact can come back with a new role.

OAB was first a triangle earned by construction.

Now OAB is also one member of the centre-boundary catalogue.

The proof has not disappeared. It has become usable structure.

The other six centre-boundary triangles use O and non-neighbouring boundary stations. They belong to the catalogue too. I must not ignore them because they are not the first beauty. A catalogue is not a display of favourite cases. It must account for the whole finite task.

Now remove O.

Choose three from A, B, C, D, E, F.

There are twenty boundary triples.

Again, the count is not the classification. Twenty says how many boundary selections must be accounted for. It does not tell me what kind of triangles they are.

The boundary stations are not an unordered pile. They lie in cyclic order. A selected boundary triple divides the six-step boundary into three gaps. Those gaps must add to six.

Only three gap patterns occur, once rotation and reversal are ignored:

1,1,4.

1,2,3.

2,2,2.

The first pattern chooses three consecutive stations.

A, B, C.

B, C, D.

C, D, E.

D, E, F.

E, F, A.

F, A, B.

There are six of these.

They form the consecutive-station family. Later the angle names may be used more fully, but the important thing here is the gap: one step, one step, four steps back. The family is not named because the eye likes the shape. It is named because the boundary order has forced the pattern.

The second pattern is 1,2,3.

This pattern includes a diameter-standing relation. One side stands on opposite stations, and the third point sits elsewhere on the boundary.

There are twelve of these.

They are important, but they must not be spent too quickly. They press toward the right-angle family, but the right angle has not yet been locked on this page. The catalogue may locate them. It may not yet use everything they will later give.

Again:

notice is allowed.

Premature use is not.

The third pattern is 2,2,2.

This chooses alternating stations.

A, C, E.

B, D, F.

There are two of these.

They form the alternating-station family. They are larger than the central equilateral triangles, but their identity is still governed by the same question: what relation is preserved across the chosen stations?

The boundary catalogue now closes:

six consecutive-station triangles,

twelve diameter-standing candidates,

two alternating-station triangles.

6 + 12 + 2 = 20.

The count returns.

Nothing has been lost.

Nothing has been smuggled in.

The full triple catalogue can now be stated:

thirty-five triples in all,

three degenerate straight triples,

thirty-two genuine triangles,

fifteen triples using O,

twelve genuine centre-boundary triangles,

twenty boundary triangles,

three boundary families.

But the numbers alone are not the lesson.

The lesson is that selection is not understanding.

Selection opens the work. Inspection decides what has been opened. Proof decides what may be spent. Count prevents the mind from replacing the finite object with a few examples.

I think this is why examples can be dangerous.

One triangle may be true and still not stand for all triangles.

One beautiful case may be memorable and still not account for the catalogue.

One central triangle may be proven and still not exhaust the field of triples.

A reader can be persuaded by an example before the cases have returned.

The page refuses that.

It asks:

How many?

Which collapse?

Which remain?

Which use the centre?

Which belong only to the boundary?

Which family?

Which proof is already earned?

Which proof is still waiting?

If I answer these questions, the figure becomes shareable in a different way. Not only as something another person can see, but as something another person can check without trusting my preference.

The straight triples show that a failed expectation may still leave a lawful result.

The centre-boundary triangles show that an earlier proof can become later structure.

The boundary triangles show that order can govern identity.

The gap patterns show that a family is not a mood or resemblance, but a relation surviving across instances.

This word instance matters.

ABC is one instance.

BCD is another.

CDE is another.

They are different triangles, but they may belong to the same family because the same gap pattern survives.

ACE and BDF are different instances too, but both preserve the alternating pattern.

A triangle may change its name and keep its family.

A selection may move around the boundary and preserve its identity.

So I must not ask only, Is it the same triangle?

I must ask:

same in what way?

Same selected points?

Same gap pattern?

Same side condition?

Same angle family?

Same constructional route?

Same proof still waiting?

Every catalogue seems to teach the same discipline in a stricter form.

Do not say triangle before testing for straightness.

Do not say family before accounting for the pattern.

Do not say all before the count returns.

Do not say right angle before the diameter relation has earned it.

Do not spend tomorrow's proof on today's page.

This is the cleanest thing geometry gives me.

It makes refusal public.

In ordinary speech, a person can say a sentence so smoothly that refusal becomes impolite. The listener may feel the claim has landed before its route has been walked. But here the page permits refusal. It even requires it.

No, these three points do not make a triangle.

No, this family is only located, not yet fully locked.

No, this example is not the catalogue.

No, this resemblance is not identity.

No, this count is not yet classification.

Each no protects the yes that remains.

The thirty-five triples are not a crowd.

They are a finite field of possible claims.

The catalogue is the discipline by which those claims become accountable.

If a thought is to survive another mind's inspection, it must allow the other mind not only to follow its affirmations, but to see where it refused to go.

That may be what makes a proof merciful.

It does not ask another person to submit to my certainty.

It gives them the refusals by which certainty was earned.
